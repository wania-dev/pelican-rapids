Impact of Social Media on PAF’s Student Culture

Connectivity or Isolation?

Have you ever walked past a schoolmate, wanting to connect with them, but hesitating to strike up a conversation? Well, that’s what social media was made for! According to the recent global demographic data in 2023, approximately 4.9 billion people are active on social media - so there’s a high chance that you may find your colleagues on there. But does it genuinely promote connectivity, or does it inadvertently contribute to student isolation?

Social media can be a medium for a school to share student insights, encouraging student connectivity. Students can gain access to real-time updates regarding valuable events and activities. However, this constant stream of information can lead to the daunting “fear of missing out”, where a student might be willing to attend every event, possibly neglecting their studies in the process.

While the benefits of social media are undeniable, limiting the constant comparison with others on social media can help in healthy connectivity between peers. If everybody starts to compare themselves with others, nobody would truly be themselves. Furthermore, the ultimate outcome depends on the school’s ability to meet the needs of both socially active and less active students - potentially developing a diverse and healthy colleague relationship.

Israel Hamas conflict

A war which has been going on for 75 years now.

On 7 October 2023, once again, the flames of war are re-ignited between Palestine and Israel, two nations claiming possession of one land, with the actions of (Harakat Al Muqawa Al-Islamiya or Hamas by its acronym).

The conflict began in 1947 following World War II. Numerous Jewish refugees were allowed to settle in Palestine. The 1947 United Nations Partition Plan for Palestine and Israel was never implemented and provoked the 1947-1949 Palestine War. Tensions arose as Jewish immigrants, driven by Zionist ideals, sought to establish a homeland in what they believed to be their ancestral land.

From the 1950s, it was perceived as a struggle between Israel and Pan-Arab nationalism, which regarded Israel as a bridgehead of western imperialism, designed to splinter Arab territorial integrity and prevent Arab unity. In addition, it became an arena of the Cold War between the West and the Soviet Union.

The Islamization of the conflict portrays it as a battle between two rival religions, Islam and Judaism, or between two opposing absolutes. The current Israeli-Palestinian status quo began following the Israeli military occupation of the West Bank and Gaza.

Hamas, the Islamic force founded as the underground wing of the Muslim brethren movement in the Gaza Strip at the beginning of the Palestine uprising in December 1987. Over the years it has been both a political and military party working towards an independent state of Palestine against Israel. The have been at war since 2006 with ceasefire on occasions with foreign help.

On 7 October 2023, Hamas organized a surprise attack on the borders of Israel. New reports death of 1400 Jews and captives taken by Hamas. Israel made a quick and brutal response by launching heavy air strike missiles on the Gaza strip, killing civilians in the process. The death toll in Gaza stands at 3,478 killed while 12,065 others have been wounded. In the occupied West Bank, 69 people have been killed and 1,300 wounded.

As of today Israel's defense minister has told field troops to be ready to enter the Gaza Strip, promising they will see the besieged Palestinian region "from the inside". The Israeli bombardment of Gaza continues, with deadly Israeli raids on the Jabalia refugee camp and a Greek Orthodox church in central Gaza City.

Neighboring Islamic nations are taking part as, fighting along the border between Israel and Lebanon also has intensified, with one Lebanese civilian killed in cross-border fire, according to the UN.

Amid signs Egypt is preparing for first aid deliveries into Gaza via the Rafah border crossing, no clear timeline has emerged. Attacks on bases housing US troops in Iraq and Syria have underscored the threat of a wider escalation.

The US also proclaims it shot down missiles "launched from Yemen" over the Red Sea that may have been targeting Israel. At least nine Palestinians were killed by Israeli forces in the currently occupied West Bank, with more captives. That brings the number of Palestinians killed by Israeli troops in the West Bank since the Gaza war began on October 7 to at least 75. The total death tolls have reached 5000 in Gaza.

Israel has vowed to "wipe out" Hamas once and for all, with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu promising to "defeat them to death." Israel Defense Forces, or IDF, will begin a field invasion of the region, with analysts predicting little to no mercy will be shown as Israel strives to "crush and destroy" what it says are Hamas strongholds. On the other hand, Hamas seeks to make Palestine an independent state again and is dedicated to resisting Israeli occupation and securing Palestinian rights.

Is The PAF Chapter College Experience Different for New-Admissions Than Long-Time PAF-ians?

College acts as a journey of self-discovery, helping pupils find their true calling, thereby acting as a road to personal growth; but going through this major life change in an all-new environment can be unnerving, irrespective of the fact that you are an extrovert or not.

To put this into view, it can be said that a student who attends PAF Chapter for college specifically would have an entirely different experience than an individual who has been on campus ever since pre-school.

Being someone who came to PAF Chapter for college specifically, I can relate to the feeling of not belonging or being able to fit in. Seeing my acquaintances exchange hugs and gossiping on the very first day, while I stand back idly watching and smiling sheepishly; the social anxiety can easily get the better of anyone.

Moreover, academically, having no one to rely on can be a challenge. Not knowing who to contact for try-outs for your favorite sport, being clueless about which office to visit for your fee challans, to see your counselor or to simply get added to the colleges’ official group chat can be a hassle.

Comprehending social events like Hypercube and Contest One can be difficult to track if you are unaware of who to talk to. Not to mention, how frustrating it can be not being able to blend in when your friends rant about the O Level Head Mistresses or why the basement was closed off years ago.

On the other hand, someone who has been in Chapter might find it much easier to blend, especially socially. The sense of comfort from seeing familiar faces and having well-stablished connections also comes in handy.

Knowing which one of your friends you’re going to sit with in all your breaks, not having to fit in and having that one special spot on campus which you can call yours, is something no new student can beat.

However, it is true that new admissions have a better chance of initiating better connections, given to the fresh start they opt for; thereby providing them with a chance to pursue unique opportunities. The reality of not achieving that feeling of personal fulfilment in a new environment always seems to stand out.

Though it can be argued that long-time PAF-ians too are deprived of experiences like exposure to obstacles and gaining communicative skills, given that they choose to surround themselves amongst the same people for years; possibly because they refuse to let go of the comfort, they have built up in all those years like pieces of a jigsaw.

In the end, I’d like to conclude this discussion on the note that everyone, be them new admissions or long-time PAF-ians; has their own fears and challenges that they are afraid to overcome. And maybe to an extent, everyone is a victim to the Outsiders’ Syndrome; striving to fit in but simultaneously trying to be different. Perhaps, not fitting in, is the new way of fitting in - the new normal.

The Kremlin was confident that Ukrainians, especially in the eastern part of the country, would welcome Russian forces as liberators. This optimism was fueled by pre-war surveys indicating a significant number of Ukrainians willing to accept some form of reintegration into a Russian sphere of influence. The belief was so strong that it led to an expectation of an easy and quick victory.

[Source: New York Times - "Drones Are Here to Stay, and Your Local Fire Department Is Getting Ready," by Thomas Gibbons-Neff, March 18, 2022]

Ethical Implications of AI and Machine Learning

What Students Should Consider?

The world of education is constantly developing and now, even more so with the introduction of AI in terms of learning, teaching, accessibility, convenience and knowledge. While the enhancement in these areas is evident, Artificial Intelligence can pose great threat to a few collective ethical boundaries.

AI systems may adversely affect or overpower the authority, privacy, control and agency of students and teachers, influencing their ability to make definite choices, implement control over their learning processes, express their creativity, or develop critical thinking skills. This then causes over reliance on the use of AI and a constant dependency on it to be a bridge between students and academic success, while students also become susceptible to potential risk of losing data. For example, AI systems may provide recommendations or advice that is not aligned with the learners’ goals, interests, or values, and they may replace human guidance with automated responses, which may not even be valid. Further, as the system collects, stores and processes private information, vulnerable groups are exposed to exploitation. Hence, the right to freedom of thought, expression, privacy and independency is violated. Ultimately, this leads to the trust, confidence, motivation, engagement, and evaluation of students to be compromised. Though temporarily, the task at hand would turn out almost perfect.

For these issues to be resolved, students should set out an ethical framework of codes of conduct, such as UNESCO published the first-ever global standard on AI ethics – the ‘Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence’ in November 2021, and implement it as such that data quality assurance, consultation and freedom of thought is observed. Further, curriculum integration, media literacy etc. can also help to spread awareness and overall, raise the ethical literacy and competence of students. By following an ethical framework, we can ensure that AI in education works for the good of humanity, individuals, societies, and the environment.
